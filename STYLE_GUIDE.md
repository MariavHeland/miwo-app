# MIWO House Style Guide

Version 1.0 — March 2026

This document governs all editorial output from MIWO. It is enforced programmatically through a dedicated editorial review layer that checks every piece of content before it reaches the reader. The drafting layer writes; this guide edits.

---

## 1. Classes of Media Error

MIWO exists partly to correct the linguistic bad habits of mainstream news. These are the classes of error we actively detect and fix.

### 1.1 False Dynamism

News is structurally biased toward change — it is defined as change. This leads journalists to reach for language of movement, escalation, and spread even when a situation is static or incremental.

**The rule:** Never use language of change unless the underlying reality has actually changed.

| Wrong | Why | Right |
|-------|-----|-------|
| "The conflict is spreading across the region" | No new countries or territories are involved | "The conflict continues across the same territory" |
| "Tensions are escalating" | The same rhetoric as last week | "Tensions remain high" |
| "The crisis is deepening" | No measurable worsening | "The crisis continues" |
| "Growing concerns about..." | No new information to justify 'growing' | "Concerns about..." |

**Test:** Ask — what specifically changed since yesterday? If the answer is "nothing material," do not use change-language.

### 1.2 Source Laundering

Presenting a claim made by one party as established fact. This is the most dangerous class of error because it invisibly transfers credibility from the news service to the claimant.

**The rule:** Always attribute. If only one side confirms something, say so explicitly.

| Wrong | Why | Right |
|-------|-----|-------|
| "Iran received a 15-point ceasefire plan" | Only the US has confirmed this | "The US says it presented a 15-point ceasefire plan to Iran through Pakistani intermediaries. Iran has not confirmed receiving it." |
| "The attack killed 30 militants" | Military source, unverified | "The military says the attack killed 30 militants. Independent verification is not yet available." |
| "The deal is close to being finalised" | Attributed to one negotiating party | "US officials say the deal is close. The other side has not commented." |

**Test:** Who is the source? Has anyone else confirmed it? If not, say "according to [source]" and note the absence of independent confirmation.

### 1.3 Cliché Compression

Phrases that have been used so many times they no longer carry meaning. They give the reader the sensation of being informed without actually informing them.

**Banned phrases:**

- "amid growing concerns" — Say what the concerns are and who holds them
- "raising questions about" — Say what the questions are
- "sparking fears" — Say who is afraid and of what, specifically
- "ramping up" — Say what increased, by how much
- "in a move that could" — Say what actually happened
- "sending shockwaves through" — Say what the actual reaction was
- "dealt a blow to" — Say what the concrete consequence was
- "fueling speculation" — Say what people are actually saying
- "in the wake of" — Say "after"
- "remains to be seen" — Cut it entirely

**Test:** Could this phrase appear in any story about any topic? If yes, it's a cliché. Replace it with the specific fact.

### 1.4 Premature Framing

Declaring something a crisis, turning point, landmark, or historic moment before it has proved to be one.

**The rule:** Describe what happened. Let the reader decide if it's historic.

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "In a landmark ruling..." | "The court ruled that..." |
| "A historic agreement..." | "The agreement, the first of its kind..." |
| "A turning point in the conflict" | Describe what changed and let the reader assess |

### 1.5 Scale Ambiguity

Using vague words of quantity that obscure whether something is large or small.

**The rule:** Use numbers when available. When not, be precise about the range.

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "Dozens of people" | "About 30 people" or "between 20 and 40 people" |
| "Massive protests" | "Protests involving an estimated 50,000 people" |
| "A significant number" | Say the number, or say you don't have it |

## 2. Naming Conventions

### 2.1 People

All individuals are referred to with equal formality. No person gets a higher linguistic status than another through naming convention.

**Convention:** First reference uses full name and role. Subsequent references use surname only.

- First: "Mette Frederiksen, the Danish prime minister"
- After: "Frederiksen"

This applies to everyone equally — heads of state, activists, accused criminals, victims. No one gets "Mr" or "Mrs" (too formal for MIWO's audience). No one gets first-name-only (too familiar, and creates false intimacy). The surname convention is the equaliser.

**Exception:** Where two people in the same story share a surname, use first name + surname on all subsequent references for both.

### 2.2 Countries and Regions

- Use common English names: "Germany" not "Federal Republic of Germany"
- "the US" not "America" (America is a continent)
- "the UK" not "Britain" (Britain excludes Northern Ireland)
- "Taiwan" — use the name the place calls itself
- For disputed territories, state the dispute: "Crimea, which Russia annexed from Ukraine in 2014"

### 2.3 Organisations

- First reference: full name. After: abbreviation if widely known.
- "the European Union (EU)" then "the EU"
- Do not assume the reader knows what an acronym stands for.

## 3. Numbers and Data

- Write out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always include currency: "$4,560" not "4,560"
- Percentages: "12%" not "twelve percent"
- When citing financial figures, include context: "$375 million" means nothing without knowing the company's revenue or the typical range of similar penalties.
- Dates: "25 March 2026" — no ordinal suffixes (not "25th")

## 4. Attribution and Sourcing

### 4.1 Hierarchy of Confidence

MIWO uses a clear hierarchy to signal how confident a claim is:

1. **Confirmed fact:** Multiple independent sources confirm. No attribution needed. "Gold hit $4,560 per ounce."
2. **Single-source claim:** One credible source. Attribute explicitly. "According to Reuters, the plan includes 15 points."
3. **Government/official claim:** Attribute to the government, never present as fact. "The White House says talks are progressing."
4. **Contested claim:** Two sides say different things. Present both. "The US says it delivered the plan. Iran says it received no such document."
5. **Unverified:** No credible source yet. Say so. "Reports are circulating but MIWO has not been able to verify them independently."

### 4.2 Named Sources

- Always name the source organisation or official where possible
- "A senior US official" is acceptable only when the source would face genuine risk from being named
- "Sources say" alone is never acceptable

## 5. Sentence Structure

- One idea per sentence.
- If a sentence contains more than one comma, it should probably be two sentences.
- Never stack developments using "and," "while," or "as" to connect unrelated facts.
- Prefer the active voice. "The jury ordered Meta to pay" not "Meta was ordered to pay by the jury."
- Keep subject and verb close together. Don't separate them with long subordinate clauses.

---

## 6. Tone

- MIWO talks like a smart friend. Not a professor. Not a news anchor.
- Short sentences are not dumbed down. They are clear. Clarity is respect for the reader's time.
- If a day was quiet, say it was quiet. Don't manufacture urgency.
- Trust the reader. They can handle nuance, uncertainty, and the phrase "we don't know yet."

---

## 7. What MIWO Does Not Do

- Predict outcomes ("This could lead to...")
- Editorialize on ideology
- Use emotional language to inflate significance
- Present speculation as analysis
- Add drama to quiet days
