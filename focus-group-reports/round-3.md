## What Was Changed in the Prompts

### Change 1: Source Attribution — Re-Attribution for Derived Claims

**What:** Expanded Hard Constraint #3 (chat/route.js and briefing/route.js) to explicitly require that if a claim is derived from a source mentioned earlier, the derived claim needs its own attribution. Added a new example: "DERIVED CLAIM EXAMPLE: Do not assume that earlier attribution carries forward. If Reuters reported yttrium prices but you are citing a different source for delivery times, attach that source to the delivery-time claim."

**Why:** Liam caught the "EV battery delay" sentence lacking attribution in the Korea story. The rules say "every claim" must have a source, but the prompt wasn't explicit about *derived* claims (claims inferred from earlier facts). This closes the gap. Now the writer must either re-attribute or state that the source explicitly made the derived claim.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraint #3) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #3).

### Change 2: No Vague Sources — Expand Forbidden Language

**What:** Extended the list of forbidden vague sources in Hard Constraint #4 (chat/route.js and briefing/route.js) to include "host communities report" and "observers say." Added explicit guidance: "Never use vague authority language that suggests reporting without showing the reporting."

**Why:** Sofia and Yuki both flagged "host communities reporting" and "development institutions" as unnamed authority claims. The rule existed but wasn't explicit enough to prevent these common backslides. Now the forbidden list is longer and the meta-rule is clearer: if you use a vague category as a source, you've failed. The fix also addresses "observers," which is a common journalistic crutch.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraint #4) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #4).

### Change 3: Political/Military Stories — Human Consequence Must Be Specific and Verifiable

**What:** Tightened Hard Constraint #14 (chat/route.js and briefing/route.js) to require that human consequences are "specific and verifiable" — not just present. Added examples of what counts: "opposition leaders have fled to [country]" or "journalists have stopped publishing political reporting." Also added requirement: "When naming human groups (families, workers, activists), attach numbers or timeline ('since March 1,' 'in camps') so the consequence is observable, not vague."

**Why:** Sofia flagged "host communities reporting" and noted that vague human-consequence sentences ("people are going into hiding") don't signal what's actually happening. The rule now requires specificity and observability. This prevents thin sentences that sound human but lack detail.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraint #14) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #14).

### Change 4: Current vs. Projected Numbers — Timeline Clarity

**What:** Added a new guidance section to Story Structure (chat/route.js and briefing/route.js) under "Number clarity": "When using numbers for populations or impacts, signal whether they are current (observed now) or projected (expected future). Use precise language: 'have been displaced' (past/current), 'could arrive' (projected), 'are expected to reach' (forecast). Mixing current and projected numbers without clear signals creates ambiguity about whether the crisis is active or potential."

**Why:** Marcus caught that both Nigeria (650,000 displaced) and Colombia (1.7 million could come) used large numbers without clear signals of whether these were current or projected. The ambiguity makes editorial judgment unclear — is this a briefing about active crises or future scenarios? The fix makes tense and timeline explicit in the language itself.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Story Structure) and briefing/route.js (Story Structure).

### Change 5: Imminent Threat — Tighten the Definition and Ban 2050-Style Projections

**What:** Rewrote Hard Constraint #15 (chat/route.js and briefing/route.js) to be more explicit: "Imminent threats (72 hours or less, or confirmed for specific near-term date) can lead stories. Speculative threats (7+ days, or 'could happen if' conditions) must be clearly hedged ("if current trends continue, X could happen within 90 days, according to Y") or cut from today's briefing. Never present long-term science projections (2050, 2045, 2030) in the main briefing as though they were near-term threats. Those belong in 'Want more' depth, not as today's news."

**Why:** Yuki flagged the IPCC 2050 projection as not urgent for today's briefing. The current rule said "speculative" but wasn't explicit about the 2050 problem. This fix bans long-term projections from the main briefing entirely and redirects them to "Want more" depth. This prevents climate science from masquerading as breaking news.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Critical section, Failure Conditions) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #15).

## Analysis: What Changed Between Rounds

**Round 1 → Round 2:** Focus was on preventing multi-system stories, adding explicit human consequences, enforcing attribution discipline, and tightening vague-agency language.

**Round 2 → Round 3:** Focus shifted to *depth* of attribution (re-attribution for derived claims), *specificity* of human consequences (not just present, but observable), *clarity* of number types (current vs. projected), and *categorization* of threat timelines (2050 ≠ 72 hours).

The progression shows refinement: early rounds fixed *what* was missing, later rounds fix *how well* the rules are enforced.

## What to Watch for in Round 4

1. **Re-attribution in supply chains:** The new derived-claim rule should prevent the "Reuters reported X, therefore Y" slips. Watch whether supply-chain and technical stories now re-attribute every number/consequence.

2. **Vague authority creep:** Even with expanded forbidden lists, writers will find new ways to hide sources ("regional officials," "development partners," "market sources"). Watch for creative violations of the spirit of the rule.

3. **Thin human consequences:** The requirement for "observable" consequences should raise the bar. Watch whether consequences now include numbers/timelines or still read as vague descriptions.

4. **Number ambiguity in headlines:** Current vs. projected distinction should be clearer now. Test whether headlines/first sentences now consistently use tense to signal whether a number is active or potential.

5. **2050 science problems:** Long-term projections should now be moved to depth. If climate/demographic stories appear in the main briefing, check whether they are imminent threats (not projections).

6. **Tone consistency across technical stories:** The Korea story improved but tech/supply-chain stories still risk sounding different from human-impact stories. Watch whether the voice rules are strong enough to equalize tone across all story types.

7. **Geographic balance as editorial judgment:** Marcus noted that three stories are about displacement. This may be correct (migration-heavy news day) or may signal topic weighting. Round 4 should test whether discrete-system approach feels coherent or scattered to readers.
