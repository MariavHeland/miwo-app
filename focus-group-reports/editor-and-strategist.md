## PART 1: EDITORIAL CRITIQUE

The briefing output shows a system that has learned the *language* of good journalism but not yet its *discipline*. The prompts are sophisticated—perhaps the most detailed editorial rules I've seen in any system. But they are **enforced as suggestions, not as hard constraints.** The result reads like a briefing written *about* MIWO's values, not *by* someone who holds them.

### What Is Actually Working

The system's first instinct is sound. The opening image of families sleeping in cars—that's genuine reporting instinct. The rule to begin with human groups rather than institutions is there, and it *sometimes* works. The demand for named sources is right. The prohibition on loaded language exists and is clear.

The system also shows geographic reach: Ethiopia, Egypt, Kenya, Yemen, the Gulf, Bahrain, the US. That's the world. The desire to show consequences, not just facts—that's correct.

### Where It Collapses: Three Structural Failures

**1. HARD CONSTRAINTS ARE NOT HARD.**

The briefing violates the "one system per story" rule in at least three places:

- Story 1 is three stories (Ethiopia fuel crisis, Egypt energy curfew, Iran-Israel war). These are completely different systems affecting different countries through different mechanisms. The rule says: "If you see 'also,' 'meanwhile,' 'separately,' you have two stories." There are no connectors here, but the underlying structure is still aggregation. A fuel shortage in Addis Ababa has nothing structurally to do with a shop curfew in Cairo. Grouping them under "the war" is lazy—the fuel shortage and the curfew are *effects* of the war, but effects on different systems, and therefore different stories.

- Story 3 is also three: UN task force (governance), Kenyan tea (commodity trade), aluminum damage (industrial production). Three distinct supply chains. Three distinct industries. Three consequences of one crisis. Fine—but then they should be told separately or the story should pick ONE.

- Story 5 (Trump protests) uses the banned words "fury," "authoritarian bent," and "law-trampling" without quotes. These are opinion words. The prompt forbids them. They appeared anyway.

- Story 6 is celebrity news (Tiger Woods DUI). The prompt says: "HARD REJECT celebrity news. No athletes...unless the story involves a system affecting millions. 'Tiger Woods arrested for DUI' is not MIWO content. EVER." It should never have been selected.

The problem is not the rules. **The problem is that violations are being allowed to pass through the editorial review.** Either the review is not running, or the model is ignoring it. The editorial review prompt is explicit: "If a story fails ANY of these tests, REJECT the story and rewrite it before output." That's not happening.

**2. VAGUE SOURCES ARE INFILTRATING.**

"Trade officials said" appears in the tea story. "UN News" appears in the humanitarian story (that's a news wire, not a source). Earlier versions mention "reports suggest" and "observers say." These hide the actual source and let readers evaluate neither the claim nor the credibility. A reader cannot weigh a claim if they don't know who made it, what their incentive is, or whether they have access to the information.

The rule exists: "HARD REJECTION FOR VAGUE SOURCES. Do not use 'sources say,' 'officials say,' 'trade officials report,' 'analysts say.' If you use any of these, REJECT the story." The rule is clear. It's being ignored.

**3. HUMAN CONSEQUENCE IS MISSING OR VAGUE.**

The humanitarian story is the most egregious violation. "More than 2.2 million children under five are acutely malnourished" — but we don't know where. Yemen? Syria? South Sudan? Which country? The story leads with NUMBERS, not with PEOPLE. It's a statistics sheet, not reporting.

The Egypt story says "the government ordered shops to close" but doesn't show what that *means* to shop workers until late in the sentence. The prompt says: "The first sentence states what is happening to people. Not what a government decided." This story buries the human consequence.

And several stories fail the fourth-sentence test: "what happens next for THESE people?" The briefing promises that every story must answer "and then what?" This is not optional. It's the difference between reporting and aggregation. Several stories don't answer it clearly.

### Tone and Voice

Where the system works, it works because of specificity. "Families in their cars" is good because we can see it. "Ethiopia's state refinery has cut output by half" is good because it's a fact with a named actor. The problem stories are the ones that get abstract: "tensions continue to rise" (attribution?), "creating two separate economies" (interpretation), "raising questions" (cliché).

The voice wavers. Some sentences sound like Reuters. Others sound like a wire service summary. The rule says: "Consistent voice. Every story must sound like the same writer." They don't.

## PART 3: WHAT'S DIFFERENT?

**Hard constraint enforcement:** Each story is one system. One consequence thread. No aggregation.

**Sourcing discipline:** Every claim has a named source attached to *that claim.* No "trade officials said"—it's "Port Authority of Mombasa." No "UN News"—it's "OCHA" or the specific agency. When I write "commodity analysts at Reuters," that's a real source, not a hedge.

**Human consequence as story structure:** Every story answers: "What happens next to these people?" Egypt story: workers shift to daytime or lose hours. Kenya story: potential job cuts in rural regions. Aluminium story: prices rise, supply tightens, sectors slow down.

**Geographic clarity:** Every first sentence names the country or specific location. No "the region," no vague geographies.

**Tense discipline:** The rewritten briefing separates observation (what happened) from consequence (what comes next). You can tell the difference. You know what is current, what is expected, what is speculative.

**No loaded language:** The rewrite removes "tensions continue to rise" (whose analysis?), "vent fury" (opinion), "authoritarian bent" (unquoted opinion word). Facts carry their own weight.

**Consistency:** All six stories sound like the same writer. Calm. Precise. Human. No shifts into wire-service mode or editorial tone-shifting.

## PART 4: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATION

Maria: The system is a **rules engine with weak enforcement.** You have written the Magna Carta of editorial standards. But you're not enforcing it. The model is allowed to *suggest* that it will follow the rules, then output violations anyway.

Here is what I would do next:

### The One Thing That Matters Most

**Build a HARD REJECTION GATE that runs AFTER the briefing is written but BEFORE it is sent to the user.**

Right now, the editorial review prompt says: "If a story fails, rewrite it." But rewriting is optional. The model can decide a violation is "minor" and let it through.

Create a second pass—a **REJECTION GATE**—that scans the completed briefing and checks:

1. **System Count:** Does each story cover exactly one system? (The lead is exception.) Count distinct actors, countries, industries, time horizons. If more than one: STOP. Do not send. Flag for rewrite.

2. **Source Check:** Does every claim have an explicit, named source attached to it? Run regex for "officials," "sources," "reports," "analysts," "observers" (without attribution). If found: STOP. Do not send. Flag.

3. **Loaded Language:** Scan for the banned words (fury, outrage, authoritarian, law-trampling, regime, slammed, blasted, lashed out, doubled down, sparked, fueled, rocked, gripped) outside quotation marks. If found: STOP. Do not send. Flag.

4. **Story Structure:** Does every story (except the lead) open with a human group + location + condition? Is there a fourth sentence showing human consequence? If missing: STOP. Do not send.

5. **Geographic Specificity:** Every first sentence names a country or city. No "the region," no abstractions. If missing: STOP.

**This gate doesn't rewrite.** It just STOPS and reports: "Story 2 has vague sources. Rewrite and retry."

The system then either rewrites automatically or returns to the user with: "The briefing failed editorial review. Retrying..." This creates transparency and forces hard constraint obedience.

### Why This Matters

Right now, you have rules but no teeth. A reader sees the briefing, trusts the system to have enforced standards, and reads a story that mixes three countries and four systems because the editorial review *suggested* it should split them but the model didn't.

With a HARD REJECTION GATE, every briefing that reaches the reader is guaranteed to have passed objective tests. You move from "we tried to follow rules" to "this output meets these standards or it doesn't go out."

### What It Unlocks

Once the gate is working, you have something powerful:

- **Credibility:** Users know the system enforces its own standards.
- **Learnable feedback:** When a rewrite fails the gate, the model sees exactly what failed and why.
- **Subscription model clarity:** You can tell subscribers: "Every briefing passes these five editorial tests before it reaches you." That's a selling point.
- **Iteration clarity:** You can measure: "What percentage of drafts fail the gate? Which tests fail most often? Are models improving at [specific constraint]?"

### Secondary: Voice Consistency

Once hard constraints are enforced, add one more thing: a **VOICE CHECK** pass that reads for tone consistency. Not perfect sameness—variation is good. But the shift between wire-service neutral and narrative warmth should be intentional, not accidental. This is less critical than the gate, but it matters for the morning-read experience.

---

## CONCLUSION

MIWO has the right values. It has the right rules. What it needs is the courage to enforce them absolutely. A briefing that violates one hard constraint is not 95% good—it's unfit to send, and the user deserves to know why.

Build the gate. Make it public. You'll go from "a system with smart rules" to "a system that reliably produces standards-compliant journalism." That's the difference between a product and a platform.
