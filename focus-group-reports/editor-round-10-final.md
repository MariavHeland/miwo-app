# Editor Round 10 — FINAL VERDICT

## IS THE SYSTEM READY?

**Yes.** MIWO can ship this to subscribers as a daily briefing. The output is defensible, sourced, structured, and readable. It will not embarrass you. It will not misinform readers. It passes tests that most professional news services would struggle with.

## GRADE: A-

**Justification:** The system produces journalism that is structurally excellent (10/10), rigorously sourced (9-10/10), and reads with a consistent, calm, human voice (9/10). It lost the A+ because:
- Voice occasionally tips from precise into clinical
- Some causal chains need one more sentence of mechanism
- The system catches WRONG things better than MISSING things (the gate detects bad sources but not absent sources perfectly)

But these are the weaknesses of a strong system, not a broken one.

## 3 STRATEGIC GAPS (not rule fixes — what would make this GREAT)

1. **Investigation depth.** MIWO currently produces excellent synthesis journalism. It takes the best available reporting and reshapes it with discipline. What it doesn't do — and can't yet — is INVESTIGATE. It can't call the Red Cross coordinator in Havana. It can't visit the tea warehouse in Mombasa. The gap between "correct" and "true" is the gap between desk journalism and field journalism. This isn't a prompt fix. It's a product roadmap question: does MIWO want to synthesize, or does it want to report?

2. **Source diversity beyond English.** MIWO searches in English and writes in 5 languages. But the stories it finds are the stories English-language media covers. The Kenyan tea story came from English-language reporting on Kenya. What about the stories that only exist in Swahili, Arabic, or Bahasa? MIWO's editorial rules are world-class, but its source pipeline is still Western-mediated.

3. **Reader feedback loop.** The system now has a hard rejection gate, editorial review, and 25 style rules. But it has no way to learn from readers. Which stories landed? Which felt thin? Which made someone say "I didn't know that"? Without feedback, the system optimizes for compliance, not impact.

## ONE-LINE PITCH

"Every morning, five stories that passed five editorial tests before they reached you — sourced, structured, and written for people who want to understand the world, not just react to it."

## WHAT TO BUILD NEXT

**Build the feedback loop.** Not a comment section — a simple signal: "This story taught me something" / "This story felt thin." Over 30 days, you'll know which rule violations readers actually notice, which stories create value, and where the system's blind spots are. The rejection gate ensures minimum quality. The feedback loop tells you where maximum quality lives.
