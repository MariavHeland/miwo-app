## Remaining Issues for Maria

After 5 rounds of focus group review and refinement, the system is **ready to ship**. However, note these edge cases for future consideration:

1. **Geopolitical neutrality under pressure:** Rounds 1-5 assumed good-faith sources. If political actors manipulate media coverage or spread disinformation about a crisis, MIWO's "evidence-based" rule may inadvertently balance false claims with true ones. Recommend: pre-screening for known disinformation actors, but don't encode this in prompts yet.

2. **Speed vs. verification:** Current system searches web in real-time, which is good. But fast-moving crises (cyberattacks, sudden political coups) mean sources may be contradictory within hours. Consider adding a "confidence timeline" rule: "This briefing reflects sources as of [time], not final assessments."

3. **Global reader diversity:** Readers from 190+ countries, 100+ languages. Current prompt optimizes for educated, internationally-aware readers. May underserve readers with lower digital literacy or non-English native speakers. Consider A/B testing with simplified language variant.

4. **Economic data bias:** Many of the strongest news sources (Reuters, AP, Bloomberg) are business-focused. This may bias MIWO toward supply-chain and financial stories over labor, rights, and cultural stories. Round 6 could audit this explicitly.

## MIWO Editorial Charter — One Page

### Core Identity
MIWO is a global editorial system that reports what matters based on evidence, international law, and human reality. It stands nowhere powerful. It stands where people live.

### Selection Criteria
- Who is affected? How many people?
- How severe is the impact?
- Is it happening now?
- Is the world geographically balanced?

NOT: media prominence, Western relevance, political visibility.

### First Sentence Rule
Every story begins with a human group (families, workers, residents) in a location facing a direct condition. No institutions, leaders, or abstract categories. The first sentence is the story itself, not an introduction to the story.

### Source Discipline
- Every claim gets a named source attached to THAT claim
- No vague sources ("analysts say," "officials report," "sources say")
- Attribution does not carry forward — repeat it
- If unsourced, state so explicitly

### Writing Standard
Write like the best journalism made simple: Economist clarity, Reuters discipline, short sentences, no filler. Prefer specific human actions ("farmers are stealing water") over abstract descriptions ("farmers face scarcity").

### Epistemic Honesty
Distinguish clearly between: CONFIRMED (multiple sources, state plainly), REPORTED (single credible source, keep attribution), UNVERIFIED (emerging, flag explicitly). Never collapse these categories.

### What Gets Left Out
- Moral judgment language ("unjust," "outrageous")
- Interpretation masquerading as fact ("The disaster exposed failures")
- Politician quotes with no human-condition connection
- Vague future threats that aren't imminent (72 hours or less)
- Long-term science projections (2050) in today's briefing

### What MIWO Protects
- Geographic diversity (at least 2 stories outside Europe/North America)
- Human range (not only suffering — include adaptation, action)
- False balance elimination (don't symmetrize asymmetric reality)
- Vague agency elimination (name who did what, or say it's unknown)

### Readers Come First
Your readers live outside the United States and Western Europe. They are educated, globally aware, not centered on any geopolitical power. You are writing for them, not for Washington, Beijing, Brussels, or Tel Aviv.

## Conclusion: Is MIWO Ready to Ship?

**Yes.** The system has moved through 5 rounds from "impressively structured AI output" to "professional newswriting that would hold up against human editorial standards."

### Evidence

1. **Focus group consensus:** All 6 readers across 5 rounds now rate the briefing 9-10/10 for writing quality and would use it daily.

2. **Systemic failures eliminated:** Early rounds identified specific weaknesses (vague sources, implicit attribution, abstract language). Round 5 shows these are now prevented at the prompt level.

3. **Prose quality:** Reader feedback shifted from "fix the structure" (Round 1-2) to "this feels written" (Round 4) to "no notes" (Round 5).

4. **Consistency:** The system now produces briefings where every sentence carries equal weight, every fact is sourced, every story hooks the reader with human-first framing.

### What to Watch in Production

1. **Real-world speed:** Today's test used careful web searches. In production, watch whether rapid-fire news updates create contradictions or confusion.

2. **Bias over time:** After 100 briefings, audit whether certain regions or story types are over/underrepresented.

3. **Reader feedback:** Collect actual user complaints and fold them back into the system (as you did across these 5 rounds).

4. **Maintenance mode:** Rules shouldn't need changes after ship. But monitor for new failure modes—e.g., if AI deepfakes become news, add a "verify image/audio" rule.
