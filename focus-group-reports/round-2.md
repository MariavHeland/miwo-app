## What Was Changed in the Prompts

### Change 1: Named sources must be attached to *every* claim, not carried forward

**What:** Expanded the attribution rule to explicitly require that claims cannot assume a source mentioned earlier still applies. Every claim gets its own "according to [named source]" or attribution.

**Why:** Feedback from Liam and Priya flagged that sentences like "Yttrium surged 140-fold, according to EU-Japan Centre. Supply chains stretch to 18 months." leave the second sentence unsourced. The reader doesn't know if 18 months came from EU-Japan Centre, Reuters, or the writer's inference. The rule now requires explicit re-attribution for every claim.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraint #3) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #3 and #15).

### Change 2: Abstract categories in sentence 1 must be replaced with named actors using active voice

**What:** Tightened the rule that sentence 1 must begin with people or institutions. Now explicitly forbids abstract categories like "manufacturers," "analysts," "engineers," or "trade officials" even if later sentences name specific actors. Sentence 1 MUST use active voice with named actors.

**Why:** The South Korea story was improved by moving from "Manufacturers are securing contracts" to "Samsung and LG signed contracts." Sofia and Marcus noted that abstract categories make stories feel generic. The rule now requires naming before action.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Story Structure, Hard Constraint #13) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #12 and #13).

### Change 3: No vague plurals for sources — "analysts," "officials," "sources" are forbidden

**What:** Extended the vague-source prohibition to explicitly list "analysts say," "three industry analysts," "sources," "officials say." These cannot stand. If you use them, name them or cut the claim.

**Why:** Priya and Marcus caught the fallback to "industry analysts" and "human rights organizations" (plural, unnamed). These sound authoritative but hide the actual source. The rule now makes this actionable failure condition.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraint #4) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #4 and #15).

### Change 4: Political and military stories must include explicit human consequences

**What:** Added a hard constraint that stories reporting institutional actions (parties dissolved, militaries attacking, coups completed) must include at least one sentence showing how people are directly affected — detained, displaced, unemployed, unable to access services, fleeing, etc.

**Why:** Sofia and Marcus both flagged that "40 parties dissolved" and "JNIM attacked" describe events but not consequences. The Guinea story improved when the prompt added "activists are going into hiding." The rule makes this mandatory, not optional.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraint #14) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraint #14).

### Change 5: Future threats must be imminent or hedged — no vague speculative language

**What:** Added explicit rule: future threats (rain, attacks, economic impacts) can only lead a story if they are imminent (within 72 hours or confirmed for specific near-term date). Vague language like "faces likelihood" or "could face" must be replaced with "if" hedging or cut entirely if not urgent for today.

**Why:** Liam and Yuki both questioned whether the Indonesia story's "forecast to receive rainfall over next five days" justified a today-briefing. The story had past crisis (earthquake), present recovery (water receding), and future risk (rain). The rule now clarifies that speculative future risk is not news unless imminent. The story should have led with the earthquakes (imminent) and mentioned rain as secondary context.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Story Structure, Failure Conditions) and briefing/route.js (Story Structure, Failure Conditions #15).

## Patterns in Reader Feedback

**Source discipline is improving but not yet complete.** Round 1 feedback focused on the absence of sources ("who said that?"). Round 2 feedback focuses on *implicit* attribution ("Is this attributed to EU-Japan Centre or Reuters?"). Readers now expect not just a source per story but a source per claim.

**Institutional stories still underweight human consequence.** Even when sources are named and facts are verified, stories about institutional actions (coups, military operations, party dissolutions) still read as summaries unless the human impact is shown in the same sentence or immediately after. "40 parties dissolved" needs "Activists are now fleeing" in the same paragraph, not as background.

**Abstract categories in sentence 1 remain tempting.** The improvement to "Samsung and LG" from "manufacturers" was visible. But "analysts," "officials," and "observers" keep appearing as subject nouns. The rule now makes this an auto-correction failure.

**Future-looking language is unclear.** Stories about *imminent* threats (earthquake happened yesterday, rain expected tomorrow) keep mixing past, present, and future without clear timeline signals. Readers report confusion about whether the crisis is active or historical.

**Geographic balance without causal connection feels scattered.** Marcus noted that the briefing felt like five unrelated crises rather than a coherent world overview. This may be by design (MIWO focuses on discrete systems, not causal chains), but it's worth noting for round 3 feedback.

---

## What to Watch for in Round 3

1. **Re-attribution in supply chain stories:** These remain the highest-risk narrative type for implicit attribution. Watch whether every claim in a tech/supply-chain story carries its own source, not just the first.

2. **Abstract sources in conflict narratives:** "Human rights organizations," "activists report," "observers say" — these are still tempting shortcuts in complex political stories. Test whether the tightened vague-source rule is being enforced consistently.

3. **Human consequence in institutional stories:** Guinea and Burkina Faso both improved when human impact was added, but the addition still felt brief. In round 3, test whether human consequences are *specific* (names, numbers, timeline) or still vague ("people are fleeing").

4. **Imminent vs. speculative timelines:** The Indonesia story showed that mixing past (earthquake), present (recovery), and future (forecast rain) creates confusion. Watch whether future threats are now clearly marked as imminent or hedged as speculative.

5. **Tone consistency in technical stories:** The South Korea story improved but still reads differently from the human-impact stories. Test whether the tightened voice rule is equalizing tone across all story types, or whether tech/supply-chain stories still drift toward corporate/policy language.

6. **Passive voice in sentence 1:** The improvement from "engineers are racing" to "Samsung signed contracts" is visible. But passive voice temptations remain ("decisions were made," "measures were taken"). Watch whether sentence 1 is now consistently active with named actors.

7. **Geographic balance with discrete systems:** Round 2 feedback noted the briefing felt scattered. This may be editorial courage (not forcing false connections), but it's worth testing whether readers find the discrete-system approach coherent or confusing.

8. **Consequence language in closing sentences:** Sentence 4 now requires "specific consequence affecting named people." Watch whether writers still default to abstract outcomes ("creating two economies," "raising concerns") or now ground consequences in observable human action.
