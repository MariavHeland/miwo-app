## What Was Changed in the Prompts

### Change 1: Sentence Rhythm Guidance — Explicit Rules for Varied Pacing

**What:** Added a new section to chat/route.js and briefing/route.js called "Prose Quality — Rhythm and Pacing" with concrete examples:
- "Vary sentence length deliberately. Not short-short-short (sounds breathless and panicked) and not long-long-long (sounds academic). Pattern: short statement (5-8 words), medium explanation (12-18 words), long consequence or detail (20+ words)."
- Example bad: "Motorists are abandoning vehicles. Gangs control territory. Police respond with force."
- Example good: "Motorists are abandoning their vehicles. Armed gangs now control 80 percent of the capital. Police have conducted at least 250 summary executions according to rights monitors."

**Why:** Multiple readers noted the prose feels more human when sentences vary in length. This is technical writing craft, not editorial philosophy. Adding it to the prompt helps the system recognize that rhythm is part of quality.

**Location:** chat/route.js (new section "Prose Quality") and briefing/route.js (new section "Prose Quality").

### Change 2: Concrete Detail Over Abstract Description

**What:** Added explicit guidance: "Prefer specific human actions over abstract descriptions. 'Farmers are stealing water from neighboring fields' is reporting. 'Farmers face water scarcity' is filler. 'Residents in single-story homes with no escape when the rains came' is writing. 'Infrastructure failures left residents vulnerable' is explanation. Always choose the first of each pair."

**Why:** Sofia and Liam both flagged that the difference between this round and earlier ones is that sentences *show* rather than *tell*. Specific human actions (abandoning, clearing, stealing, walking away) feel like reporting; abstract nouns (failures, vulnerability, impact) feel like templates. Making this explicit in the prompt helps prevent the AI from defaulting to abstract language when specific details are available.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Prose Quality section) and briefing/route.js (Prose Quality section).

### Change 3: Opening Sentences Must Hook — Concrete Image First

**What:** Added guidance to the Editorial Voice section: "The opening sentence must create a scene or image, not a summary. A scene is concrete and immediate: 'Motorists are abandoning their vehicles at checkpoints.' A summary is abstract and distant: 'Haiti's security crisis has worsened.' Scenes make readers want to read on. Summaries sound like headlines."

**Why:** Amara and Liam both mentioned opening sentences like "motorists abandoning vehicles" make them want to keep reading, while traditional news ledes don't. This is a writing-craft rule, not an editorial rule. Adding it helps prevent openings from defaulting to institutional framing.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Editorial Voice section) and briefing/route.js (Editorial Voice section).

### Change 4: No Mixed Epistemic States Within One Sentence

**What:** Added a new rule under Hard Constraints: "Do not mix current-tense observation with future-tense projection in the same sentence without clear signaling. Wrong: 'The blockade affects the medical system and strands supplies.' Right: 'The blockade affects supplies. Oxygen is expected to run out in five days, according to Lebanon's health ministry.' The reader needs to know: is this happening now or is this predicted?"

**Why:** Yuki caught that the Beirut story mixed "affects" (present, happening now) with "expected to last five more days" (future, projected). This creates epistemic confusion about whether the crisis is active or potential. The fix clarifies that readers need distinct sentences for different time states.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Hard Constraints #9) and briefing/route.js (Hard Constraints #9).

### Change 5: Narrative Arc in Longer Sentences — Temporal or Causal Closure

**What:** Added guidance: "When a sentence exceeds 18 words, it must have narrative closure. Closure means a temporal endpoint (when the rains came), a causal outcome (may cripple irrigation for years), or a contrasting detail (drilling deeper to compensate). Without closure, long sentences feel unfinished. Example with closure: 'Residents in single-story homes with no escape when the rains came.' Example without: 'The disaster exposed infrastructure failures in systems designed for lesser storms.'"

**Why:** Sofia noticed that sentences like "residents in single-story homes with no escape when the rains came" have a satisfying ending, while sentences that drift into explanation ("infrastructure failures in systems") feel generated and incomplete. This is about prose rhythm and closure, not content. Adding it helps the system understand that long sentences need endings.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Prose Quality section) and briefing/route.js (Prose Quality section).

### Change 6: Specific Observation vs. Reported Analysis — Know the Difference

**What:** Added: "If you can see it (motorists abandoning cars, families clearing mud), write it as observation. If it requires interpretation (infrastructure failure caused the disaster), attribute it or cut it. Do not present interpretations as facts. Right now, 'The disaster exposed what officials acknowledge as infrastructure failures' — this is you explaining. Rewrite: 'Officials acknowledged that insufficient drainage and uncontrolled urbanization made the disaster worse.' Or better: cut the explanation and let the facts (four times average rainfall, no escape routes) speak."

**Why:** Liam flagged a sentence that was explanation masquerading as reporting. The prompt now makes explicit that if a statement requires interpretation, it must be attributed to a source or cut. This prevents the slip from reporting into editorial voice.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Failure Conditions) and briefing/route.js (Failure Conditions).

### Change 7: Verify Opening Scenes Are Sourced

**What:** Added a check: "If sentence 1 contains a scene or image (motorists abandoning cars, families clearing mud), either it must be observed (you have a source describing this scene) or state the timeframe clearly. 'Motorists are abandoning vehicles' needs source or context. 'Since the gangs took control, motorists have begun abandoning vehicles' contextualizes the scene. Always verify that dramatic opening images are not invented atmospherics."

**Why:** Yuki questioned whether the motorist detail was March 29 news or setup narrative. The prompt now makes clear that opening scenes must be verified or contextualized, not used for atmospherics without sourcing.

**Location:** chat/route.js (Editorial Voice section) and briefing/route.js (Editorial Voice section).

## Analysis: The Shift From Rounds 1-3 to Round 4

**Rounds 1-3** focused on *structure and authority*:
- Preventing multi-system stories
- Enforcing attribution discipline
- Clarifying institutional vs. human impact
- Making vague sources explicit

**Round 4** focuses on *prose quality and reading experience*:
- Sentence rhythm and pacing
- Concrete detail over abstract description
- Opening hooks that make readers want to continue
- Narrative closure in longer sentences
- Epistemic clarity (observation vs. interpretation vs. prediction)

The progression is visible: early rounds fixed *what* the system writes, later rounds fix *how well* it writes. The prompts are now moving from editorial compliance to prose craft.

## What to Watch for in Round 5

1. **Sentence rhythm consistency:** The new rhythm guidance should prevent monotonous pacing. Watch whether stories now have natural cadence (short, medium, long) or still fall into patterns.

2. **Concrete detail vs. abstract:** The guidance on "farmers stealing water" vs. "farmers facing scarcity" should raise the specificity floor. Test whether specific human actions now dominate or whether abstract nouns still creep in.

3. **Opening scenes and sourcing:** The new requirement that opening scenes be verified should prevent atmospherics without attribution. Test whether dramatic openings now have clear sourcing or remain narrative flourishes.

4. **Epistemic clarity in time-layered stories:** The guidance on separating current observation from future projection should eliminate mixed-tense sentences. Test whether readers can now clearly distinguish "this is happening" from "this is projected."

5. **Narrative arc in long sentences:** The closure rule should prevent long sentences that drift into explanation. Test whether long sentences now feel complete or still fade into unfinished ideas.

6. **Attribution of interpretive claims:** The guidance on observation vs. interpretation should prevent editorial explanation masquerading as fact. Test whether any claims about what "exposed" or "revealed" are now sourced or cut.

7. **Proportion and editorial judgment:** Marcus noted that editorial judgment about story proportion (Haiti urgent now vs. Brazil recovery underway) should be visible in word count and emphasis. Round 5 should test whether briefing proportion now reflects editorial urgency.

8. **Thematic coherence:** Round 4 ended with five stories about scarcity/resource control. Test whether this is intentional thematic unity or accident. If intentional, the briefing's coherence is stronger. If accident, consider whether geographic and thematic diversity would be better.

## Conclusion

**Remaining gaps:**
1. Some sentences still have passive construction or abstract nouns (e.g., "waiting," "what once subsidized").

3. Mixed epistemic states (current + future) occasionally appear in the same sentence.

**Recommendation for Round 5:**
Focus on edge cases and consistency. The core writing quality is now good. Rounds 1-4 have built a system that produces clean, human-focused, fact-first reporting. Round 5 should test whether this quality holds under stress: longer briefings, more complex stories, edge cases like contested claims or future scenarios. If the system maintains this quality at scale, MIWO is ready.
